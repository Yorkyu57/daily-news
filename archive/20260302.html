    [Original] Consultation on social media ban for under-16s to begin
    A public consultation launching Monday seeks input from young people, parents, and guardians regarding a potential ban on social media for under-16s. This initiative follows Australia's decision last year to prohibit children from accessing platforms including Instagram, Snapchat, YouTube, and TikTok, making it the first nation to implement such a ban. Technology Secretary Liz Kendall stated the consultation aims to determine how young people can "thrive in an age of rapid technological change." While gauging opinion on an outright prohibition, the government is also soliciting views on less drastic interventions. To supplement public feedback, pilots will test proposed measures to generate "real-world evidence" of their effectiveness. Kendall emphasized that "the path to a good life is a great childhood, one full of love, learning and play," noting this principle applies equally to the online world. She acknowledged that parents are "grappling with how much screen time their children should have" and the impact of online content. The government plans to foster a wider debate through community events involving MPs, influencers, and schools. The consultation invites participation from parents, carers, young people, academics, civil society organizations, and industry, with tailored versions available for different groups. An academic panel will also assess evidence, including Australia's recent experience. The consultation closes on 26 May, with a government response expected "in the summer." Political support for the ban is significant. The House of Lords has voted in favor of a full ban for under-16s, and more than 60 Labour MPs have joined Conservatives and Liberal Democrats in backing the policy. In an open letter to the prime minister, Labour MPs argued that "successive governments" had done "too little to protect young people from... unregulated, addictive social media platforms." Conservative leader Kemi Badenoch has pledged to introduce a ban if her party takes power. Liberal Democrat education spokeswoman Munira Wilson warned there is "no time to waste," criticizing the consultation as potentially "kicking the can down the road." The Molly Rose Foundation, established by the family of Molly Russell who died by suicide in 2017 after viewing harmful content, welcomed the move as a "crucial opportunity to decisively strengthen online safety laws." Conversely, the NSPCC and other groups warn a full ban risks "unintended consequences," arguing it could create a "false sense of safety" as children and threats migrate elsewhere. They advocate for stronger enforcement of existing rules instead. Professor Sonia Livingstone of the London School of Economics noted that the goal should be "better safety from Big Tech companies" so children can connect online safely. Globally, scrutiny is intensifying; the EU recently ordered TikTok to change its "addictive design" or face fines, while a landmark trial in California examines the mental health effects of Instagram and YouTube.

    Five years after the collapse of Clarity, the United Kingdom's oldest social enterprise, former employee Camilla Marcus-Dew has launched a new venture, Amplify Goods, to continue the organization's social mission. Founded in 1854 to provide employment for blind people, Clarity eventually focused on soap production and counted Queen Victoria and actress Joanna Lumley among its patrons. However, the company collapsed in 2020 following a significant pension fund shortfall. Nicholas Marks purchased the business out of administration, promising to sustain operations, but instead laid off workers who were owed wages, furlough, and redundancy payments. The factory closed, leaving the company owing more than Â£400,000 to 84 employees. Marcus-Dew, who served as head of commercial, criticized Marks' lack of interest in protecting disabled jobs, stating, "He wasn't interested in growing jobs for disabled people and even protecting the jobs for the disabled people that we had working with us, which was truly heart-breaking to watch unfold." Following the closure, Marks sued Marcus-Dew and another employee, wrongly blaming them for the company's failure. The case was dismissed after a lengthy legal battle. Determined to rebuild, Marcus-Dew co-founded Amplify Goods with Pasha Michaelsen. Operating from a warehouse managed by the homelessness charity Crisis in London, the new business provides paid work experience at the London living wage to homeless individuals, disabled people, and prison leavers. "The longer you're out of employment, you start to lose confidence that you do have something to offer even just in being yourself," Michaelsen explained. Workers like Taylor, who is autistic and has verbal dyspraxia, and Jentital, who secured his first job through the program, have gained confidence and skills. Their fateful assignment was depicted in the 2018 movie A Private War, with the actor Jamie Dornan playing Conroy. The Liverpool-born photographer died from a heart attack on Saturday in Devon, where he had lived, his brother Alan told the BBC. "He did all his life what he wanted to do to make a difference - he found great pleasure in exposing wrongs," Alan added.     [Original] Why is WhatsApp's privacy policy facing a legal challenge in India?
    A landmark legal battle concerning privacy rights, data control, and the business models of major technology firms is currently unfolding in India, centering on WhatsApp's 2021 privacy policy. The country's Supreme Court is scrutinizing the policy, which previously required users to share data with parent company Meta for advertising purposes to continue using the app. This "take it or leave it" approach drew sharp criticism from the Competition Commission of India (CCI), which argued it left users with "no real choice," and from digital rights activists who labeled the policy invasive. In a recent affidavit seen by the BBC, WhatsApp informed the Supreme Court it would comply by March 16 with an order mandating greater user control over data sharing. The messaging platform stated that users could continue using the app even if they opted out of sharing data with Meta for advertising. This development follows stern warnings from the Court, which declared it would not allow WhatsApp or Meta to "play with" Indians' right to privacy or "make a mockery" of the Constitution, which guarantees privacy as a fundamental right. The Court characterized the previous policy as a method of "committing theft of private information" and directed WhatsApp to establish a consent-based framework. The legal conflict began in March 2021 when the CCI ordered an investigation into alleged "exploitative and exclusionary conduct." In November 2024, the CCI fined Meta $25 million for "abusing its dominant position," ordering behavioral remedies within three months. While the CCI barred WhatsApp from sharing user data with Meta entities for five years, a company law tribunal later stayed this specific ban while upholding the penalty. In January 2026, the firms moved the Supreme Court to contest the fine. WhatsApp's affidavit reiterated that personal messages remain protected by end-to-end encryption. It promised to notify users, allowing them to opt out of data sharing and modify choices via a prominent settings tab. "Sharing of user data collected on WhatsApp with other Meta companies... shall not be made a condition for users to access WhatsApp service in India," the affidavit stated. WhatsApp also noted it is preparing to comply with India's new digital data protection law, which faces its own Supreme Court challenge regarding free speech and surveillance concerns. Reactions remain divided. While some activists welcome the move against exploiting developing markets, digital rights activist Nikhil Pahwa argues that advertising is a legitimate business model. "If sharing data to enable better delivery of advertising on a firm's platforms is an abuse of dominance, then Gmail, Google Maps and Search should also be prevented from sharing data with Google's advertising business. Where will this end?" Pahwa asked, suggesting users uncomfortable with policies can switch to alternatives like Signal or Telegram. With approximately 853 million users in India, WhatsApp remains ubiquitous despite the existence of rivals. The Supreme Court has not yet ruled on the penalty against Meta as the case continues.

    [Original] Months of planning behind US-Israeli mission to target Iran's supreme leader
    The attack that killed Iran's Supreme Leader Ayatollah Ali Khamenei occurred in the middle of the morning rather than at night, a timing dictated by crucial intelligence received hours prior. For months, the United States and Israel monitored senior Iranian figures, anticipating a moment when they would congregate. They identified that Khamenei would be at a compound in central Tehran on Saturday morning, alongside other senior military and intelligence officials. While the exact tracking methods remain classified, US President Donald Trump hinted at their sophistication in a social media post, stating, "He was unable to avoid our intelligence and highly sophisticated tracking systems." Analysts suggest this intelligence likely stemmed from technical tracking of telecoms and mobile phone systems, a method reportedly used by Israel during the 12-day war last June to target nuclear program scientists. By tracking bodyguards and building a "pattern of life," intelligence agencies could predict movements and identify vulnerabilities. The failure of Iranian security to address these risks over months suggests either a deep counterintelligence failure or the adaptability of US and Israeli tracking methods. Additionally, Iranian officials may have calculated that a daylight attack was less probable. According to the New York Times, the intelligence originated from the CIA but was passed to Israel to execute the strike. This indicates a division of labor where Israel focuses on leadership targets while the US targets military assets. The intelligence provided sufficient forward notice to plan an attack using jets capable of firing long-range missiles. Rather than a single decapitation strike, the operation was intended to signal a wider campaign, moved forward to exploit the specific window of opportunity. Israeli jets reportedly reached Tehran in approximately two hours, though the exact firing distance remains unclear. Around 09:40 local time, the jets utilized 30 bombs to attack the compound. The high munition count may have been necessary to penetrate an underground bunker Khamenei was reportedly using, which, while protective, was not among the regime's deepest facilities. Other sites in the capital were also struck, including the office of President Masoud Pezeshkian, who later confirmed he was safe. Iran has confirmed the deaths of three senior defense officials: Defence Council secretary Ali Shamkhani, Defence Minister Brig Gen Aziz Nasirzadeh, and IRGC commander Gen Mohammad Pakpour. At the time of the strike, it was the middle of the night at Mar-a-Lago in Florida, where President Trump and top officials monitored the events. Confirmation of Khamenei's death took hours to arrive. Iran had reportedly prepared succession plans for Khamenei and other senior officials, leaving the ultimate impact of the killing on the conflict's course currently unclear.

    [Original] Ayatollah Khamenei's iron grip on power in Iran comes to an end
    US President Donald Trump has announced the death of Ayatollah Ali Khamenei, Iran's 86-year-old Supreme Leader, on the first day of massive US and Israeli air strikes. Iranian state television later confirmed the death of the ruler who had led the country for three decades. Khamenei, one of only two supreme leaders since the 1979 Islamic Revolution, held absolute power as head of state and commander-in-chief, controlling the Revolutionary Guards and vetting public policy. His death in violent circumstances heralds an uncertain future for Iran and the region. Born in Mashhad in 1939 to a religious family, Khamenei qualified as a cleric by age 11. He became a prominent critic of the Shah, enduring six arrests, torture, and exile. Following the revolution, he supported the US embassy hostage crisis, which cemented Iran's anti-Western path. In 1981, he survived a bomb assassination that permanently disabled his right arm. Later that year, he won the presidency with 97% of the vote, condemning "deviation, liberalism, and American-influenced leftists." During the eight-year Iran-Iraq War, Khamenei served as a wartime leader on the front lines, witnessing massive casualties from chemical weapons and human wave tactics. This conflict solidified his distrust of the US and West. In 1989, he succeeded Ayatollah Khomeini as Supreme Leader. Admitting he was a "minor seminarian," he nonetheless built a power base over 30 years through loyalist networks in the judiciary, media, and clerical elite. Karim Sadjadpour of the Carnegie Endowment noted his power relied on a "tight-knit cartel of hardline clergymen and nouveau riche Revolutionary Guardsmen." Khamenei maintained control through political repression, crushing protests in 1999, 2009, and 2019. He opposed gender equality, arresting and torturing women who campaigned against the hijab. Following the 2022 death of Mahsa Amini, security forces killed over 550 people and detained 20,000. Internationally, Iran was labeled part of the "Axis of Evil." While Khamenei issued a fatwa against nuclear weapons, the West suspected secret development, leading to crippling sanctions. Tensions escalated in 2025 when Israel attacked Iranian nuclear facilities, prompting missile barrages. When the US joined the strikes, Khamenei vowed never to surrender. In January 2026, economic failure sparked protests that security forces brutally suppressed, killing at least 6,488 protesters and detaining 53,700. Trump threatened further strikes unless Iran abandoned its "sinister nuclear ambitions." Khamenei refused, warning, "The Americans should know that if they start a war, this time it will be a regional war." He remained a firm, often brutal, hand on the levers of power until his death.
