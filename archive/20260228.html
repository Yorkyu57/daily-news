    Scottish craft beer giant Brewdog is poised to announce the sale of its business next week, according to an internal email from Chief Executive James Taylor. Founded in 2007 by James Watt and Martin Dickie, the company operates breweries and approximately 60 pubs in the UK, alongside international sites in the US, Australia, and Germany. The potential sale follows the appointment of consultants AlixPartners two weeks ago after the firm failed to generate profits in recent years. In the update seen by BBC Scotland News, Taylor described the situation as a "difficult decision" and apologized for the uncertainty it creates. To facilitate a smooth transition to new ownership, the company will temporarily suspend online sales, though all bars will continue trading normally over the weekend. Taylor noted there has been "a great deal of interest" from potential buyers, with a full update scheduled for a company-wide "all hands" meeting early next week. Notably, Brewdog's German arm, including its Berlin brewery and bar, will be excluded from the sale and is set for liquidation. But the Supreme Court agreed that the trial could take place in an English court, setting a precedent that British companies can be held to account in the UK for actions taken by suppliers in another country.     Prime Minister Mark Carney is currently in India for a pivotal diplomatic mission aimed at repairing bilateral ties and reducing both nations' trade reliance on the United States. The visit marks a significant turnaround in relations following a collapse triggered by Canada's 2023 accusation that India orchestrated the extrajudicial killing of Sikh separatist Hardeep Singh Nijjar in British Columbia. While India has consistently denied involvement, and four men remain charged in the case, Ottawa is now pursuing a "pragmatic" approach to foreign policy under Carney. Carney's itinerary reflects a strict focus on commerce. He began his trip in Mumbai to meet business leaders before traveling to New Delhi for talks with Prime Minister Narendra Modi. Unlike previous visits by Justin Trudeau or Stephen Harper, Carney will not visit cultural sites like the Taj Mahal or Punjab, signaling that economic interests now take precedence over community outreach. The agenda includes discussions on energy, technology, artificial intelligence, defense, and attracting talent, alongside efforts to advance a comprehensive free trade agreement stalled for 15 years. Despite the diplomatic reset, concerns persist within Canada's Sikh community regarding transnational repression. MP Sukh Dhaliwal criticized the government's stance, calling claims that India is no longer a threat "disconnected from the reality." However, Canadian officials stated that "robust" security discussions are ongoing and affirmed that Canada does not believe the Indian government is currently linked to violent crimes on Canadian soil, noting they "probably would not be taking this trip" if that were the case. Vina Nadjibulla of the Asia Pacific Foundation of Canada described the situation as requiring "delicate handling" but emphasized that the lack of a preferential trade agreement between Canada and India "adds urgency" to seize this "window of opportunity." The visit is driven by shared economic pressures, particularly tariffs enacted by US President Donald Trump, which have impacted both economies. Carney warned that the world is "in the midst of a rupture, not a transition," asserting that "if you are not at the table, you are on the menu." For India, diversifying energy imports is critical after facing steep US tariffs and pressure to stop buying Russian oil. Consequently, the two nations are likely to announce a 10-year uranium supply agreement to support India's nuclear power expansion, alongside deals for crude oil and gas. While a full trade deal may take time, these energy sectors offer immediate wins. For Modi, the visit boosts his domestic image as a leader who resisted Canadian pressure, while for Carney, it represents a strategic move to diversify Canada's trade partners in an uncertain global order.
